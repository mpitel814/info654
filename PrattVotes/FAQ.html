Frequently Asked Questions

This FAQ section is designed to give clear, simple answers to the questions first-time voters, parents, and educators ask most. Each item below expands when clicked. All information is based on reliable civic resources such as the NYC Board of Elections and Vote.gov. Our goal is to make voting guidance approachable for users of all ages and levels of digital literacy.

To register to vote in New York, you must:

  • Be a U.S. citizen
  • Be 18 years old by the date of the election
  • Be a resident of New York State for at least 30 days before the election
  • Not be currently incarcerated for a felony conviction
  • Not claim the right to vote elsewhere

New York has deadlines for several stages of the election process:

  • Voter Registration: Typically 10 days before Election Day
  • Early Voting: Begins 10 days before Election Day and runs for nine days
  • Absentee Ballot Request: Must be made at least 10 days before Election Day if done by mail
  • Absentee Ballot Return: Must be postmarked by Election Day

New York does not require most voters to show photo ID. However, first-time voters who registered by mail may need to show:

  • A driver’s license or non-driver ID
  • A utility bill
  • A bank statement
  • A paycheck or government document with your name and address

You can request an absentee ballot in New York if you:

  • Will be outside your county on Election Day
  • Have a temporary or permanent illness/disability
  • Are caring for someone who is ill or disabled
  • Are in jail for a non-felony offense
  • Have a work-related conflict

Early voting is available for nine days before Election Day. You must visit your assigned early voting site, which may differ from your regular polling place. The process is similar to Election Day voting—same machines, same ballots.

Your polling place may change due to construction, accessibility needs, or election district updates. Always check:

  • The NYC Board of Elections poll finder
  • Your mailed voter notice
  • The BOE website the week before the election

New York polling locations offer several accommodations, including:

  • Wheelchair-accessible entrances and voting booths
  • BMDs (Ballot Marking Devices) for voters with disabilities
  • Assistance from a person of your choice (except your employer or union rep)
  • Language assistance in covered languages

You have the right to request a replacement ballot—called a “spoiled ballot.” Simply bring it to a poll worker and ask for a new one.
